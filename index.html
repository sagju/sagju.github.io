I am an applied theorist broadly interested in information economics. My work has focused on two developments where the nature of information greatly affected business and policy: (1) the boom of data-intensive practices among firms and (2) the highly visible but sparsely informed management of the COVID-19 pandemic. These papers are initial steps to a broader research agenda in information design, information markets, and the economics of social data.

Research

The Visible Hand - Job Market Paper

Technological innovations have allowed some sellers to collect detailed information about buyers. We study the effects of these changes in the heterogeneous precision of sellers' demand information. Precision plays a central role in the incentive to profit through more efficient trade versus more extractive trade - impacting total surplus and its distribution. The more heterogeneous buyers' preferences and the flatter sellers' costs, the greater the disposition of imprecisely informed sellers to profit through extraction despite limiting trade. Precision relaxes this tension, allowing sellers to target extraction when it is less damaging to trade. It increases aggregate surplus and benefits (hurts) buyers with a low (high) willingness to pay. However, in alternate settings, imprecisely informed sellers are predisposed to profit through trade, and expertise can reorient them toward extraction to such an extent that it decreases the surplus of all buyers along with the aggregate. In either case, precision makes sellers who acquire it more profitable, but it can benefit or hurt other sellers. The more competitive an economy and the lower the precision of sellers who upskill, the worse the profit externalities; nevertheless, externalities can be positive and allow all sellers to become more profitable. And yet, competition can also protect precision leaders by discouraging laggards from catching up.

The Political Economy of Pandemics

The COVID-19 policy responses represent an unprecedented challenge. Policymakers worldwide, have to react fast to a totally new type of crisis under enhanced public scrutiny, as their policies are immediately compared across countries. We show how relative comparisons induce herding when policymakers are concerned by their popularity, and we discuss its extent under different scenarios. This policy contagion induce some countries to choose less efficient policies than they would in isolation or when facing a less correlated shock. Further, when shocks arrive sequentially, first movers prompt coordination on their policies of later movers, albeit inducing less distortions than when shocks are simultaneous. On average, however, the policy coordination triggered by a common pandemic shock improves welfare by disciplining biased policy agendas.
